The Spirit, Heritage & Festivals of Judaism

About Judaism

Origin: Oldest Abrahamic religion — around 2000–1500 BCE, Middle East
Belief: One God, covenant between God and the Jewish people
Sacred Text: Hebrew Bible (Tanakh) — especially the Torah
Places of Worship: Synagogues
Followers Called: Jews


Judaism emphasizes identity, memory, spirituality, and the importance of moral living. Its festivals celebrate liberation, miracles, gratitude, and centuries of cultural endurance.

Rosh Hashanah

Rosh Hashanah is the Jewish New Year and marks the beginning of the High Holy Days. It is a time of spiritual reflection, self-evaluation, and renewal, when Jews attend synagogue services, listen to the shofar (ram’s horn), and pray for a good year ahead. Traditional foods such as apples dipped in honey symbolize hopes for sweetness and blessings. Rather than loud celebrations, the mood is thoughtful and introspective, encouraging people to repair relationships, seek forgiveness, and set intentions for moral and meaningful living.


Date: 1st Ersel (First month of the Jewish Calendar)

Yom Kippur

Yom Kippur, known as the Day of Atonement, is considered the holiest day in Judaism. It occurs ten days after Rosh Hashanah and focuses on repentance, forgiveness, and reconciliation with God and others. Many Jews fast for 25 hours, refrain from work and worldly distractions, and spend most of the day in synagogue prayer services. The purpose of Yom Kippur is not punishment but spiritual cleansing — acknowledging mistakes, committing to change, and emerging renewed and morally grounded.


Date: 10th day of Tishri in the Hebrew calendar

Sukkot

Sukkot, the Festival of Booths, is a joyful, week-long harvest celebration that remembers the Israelites’ journey through the desert after leaving Egypt. During this festival, Jews build temporary outdoor huts called sukkahs, decorated with fruits and greenery, and many eat meals or even sleep inside them. The sukkah symbolizes human fragility and trust in God’s protection. Another important ritual is waving the Four Species — palm, willow, myrtle, and citron — as a sign of gratitude for nature and its blessings.


Date: 5th of Tishri
