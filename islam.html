The Spirit, Heritage & Festivals of Islam

About Islam

Origin: 7th century CE, Arabia (Makkah & Madinah)
Belief: One God (Allah), Prophethood of Muhammad (PBUH)
Holy Book: The Qur’an
Places of Worship: Mosques
Followers Called: Muslims

Islam is built on devotion, spirituality, community, and moral discipline. Its festivals reflect gratitude, sacrifice, unity, prayer, and remembrance of important moments in Muslim history.

Eid al-Fitr

Eid-ul-Fitr marks the end of Ramadan and is a celebration of faith, community, and thankfulness. The day begins with a special congregational prayer, followed by sermons and collective supplications. Muslims wear new or clean clothes, visit relatives and friends, exchange gifts, and share festive foods and sweets—especially dishes unique to each culture. Charity, known as Zakat-ul-Fitr, is obligatory and ensures that those in need can also celebrate with dignity and joy. Eid-ul-Fitr highlights forgiveness, unity, peace, and gratitude for the strength and blessings experienced during Ramadan.


Date: 1st Shawal

Eid al-Adha

Eid-ul-Adha, the “Festival of Sacrifice,” commemorates Prophet Ibrahim’s willingness to sacrifice his son in obedience to God’s command—a test of devotion and faith. The festival occurs during the Islamic month of Zhul-Hijjah and coincides with the climax of the annual Hajj pilgrimage in Makkah. Muslims begin the day with a communal prayer, then perform Qurbani—the sacrifice of an animal such as a goat, sheep, cow, or camel. The meat is divided into three parts: one for the family, one for relatives and friends, and one for the poor. Eid-ul-Adha teaches lessons of humility, generosity, surrender to God’s will, and care for the less fortunate. It is also a celebration of family, shared meals, hospitality, and spiritual reflection.


Date: 10 Zul Hajj

Ramadan

Ramadan is the ninth month of the Islamic lunar calendar and is considered the holiest month in Islam. Muslims fast from dawn to sunset, abstaining from food, drink, and negative behavior to cultivate self-discipline, patience, and empathy for the poor. The pre-dawn meal is called suhoor, and the fast is broken at sunset with iftar, traditionally starting with dates and water. Ramadan is also a time of increased prayer, Qur’an recitation, charity, and seeking forgiveness. The Night of Power (Laylat-ul-Qadr), believed to be when the Qur’an was first revealed, is observed with special devotion. Beyond physical fasting, Ramadan encourages spiritual cleansing, gratitude, generosity, compassion, and deep reflection on one’s relationship with God.


Duration: The Whole Month of Ramadan

Mawlid al-Nabi (mostly observed in South Asia)

Mawlid-un-Nabi commemorates the birth of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and honors his life, character, and teachings. While its observance varies—some Muslim communities celebrate publicly, while others focus privately—common traditions include gatherings for prayer, recitation of poetry (Naat), storytelling about the Prophet’s compassion, justice, and wisdom, and acts of charity. Homes, streets, and mosques may be decorated, and community meals may be shared. The festival encourages believers to embody the Prophet’s values—kindness, humility, honesty, service to humanity, and mercy toward all creation.


Duration: 12 Rabi Al Awal
